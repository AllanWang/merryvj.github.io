My Role

Working on a team of 8 interns and 2 mentors from Duke OIT (including 1 team lead), I was responsible for the design and front-end implementation of our project. Throughout this project, I...

  • Engaged my team in the human-centered design process by leading team brainstorming sessions
  • Created low-fidelity wireframes in Figma and created clickable prototypes in InVision
  • Implemented my designs in XCode and integrated back-end data to create a fully functioning iOS app
  • Discussed product strategy and designing for scale

The Opportunity

Hundreds of thousands of visitors arrive on Duke University's campus each year. Not only to watch games or tour campus as a prospective student, but to visit attractions like the Nasher Museum of Art and Sarah P. Duke Gardens.

We decided to make a visitor app for the Duke Gardens first. After meeting with multiple stakeholders (including Duke Athletics and one of Duke's executive VPs) , our team decided to use the gardens as a proof of concept for a visitor app that could be expanded to the rest of campus.

Did someone say 'Augmented Reality'? Besides creating a visitor app, another half of our project was focused on implementing the AR technology from our project sponsor.
A 5-week-long story short – things didn't work as expected.

My opportunity was working as a "UX team of one". Although we had a team lead from Duke OIT, my team members' roles were mostly self-directed. This meant that I wanted to incorporate elements of the design process in our work, I had to shape those opportunities myself.

Research

Field visits & interviews

My team made several trips to the Duke Gardens to observe visitors, conduct interviews with gardens staff, and to experience the gardens for ourselves. One visit included a tour of the entire gardens offered by volunteer tour guides.



We interviewed gardens staff, from tour guides to the marketing director. They shared with us some of the most commonly asked questions from visitors:

"Where's the parking lot?"

"Where's the bathroom?"

"Where should I go today?"

"How long does it take to visit?"

"How do I get out of here?

"Where can we eat lunch/dinner?"

"Where is the rose garden?" (asked while standing in the Rose Garden)

User personas

From informal conversations with gardens visitors and secondary research, I created 3 "proto-personas" to help guide the rest of our design process.


Ideation

Task analysis

When my team began thinking about which features to incorporate in our app, I took initiative in suggesting we use post-it notes to organize our ideas and try to create a "task analysis". For each key stage in the visitor journey, we added ideas about both what features and information a user would need.


Sketching together

To encourage participation and divergent thinking, I asked all of my team members to sketch out design ideas before discussing our ideas as a group.



Wireframes

From low up to medium-fidelity

Because this project could become part of Duke's suite of mobile apps, I did not define a distinct visual design. I spoke to one of Duke OIT's UX/UI designers and learned that while OIT did not have a mobile design/pattern system, they did sometimes take cues from Material Design. I kept this in mind when deciding to use cards, drop shadows, and rounded corners.



Getting immediate feedback

I asked several other interns at Duke OIT to walk through the wireframes and tell me what they thought each screen’s function was. Everyone seemed to have a good grasp of what the app did, although the concept of “saving” favorite locations sparked discussion. What if it could generate a tour from the saved places?

Development

A 4-week time crunch

Armed with 3-days worth of XCode training, I began building our app's visual components in XCode's storyboard and writing Swift code to make use of the data my teammates prepared on the back-end.

Staying flexible

We had to prioritize features that would contribute the most to a minimum viable product over those that would complicate and lengthen development. I questioned my previous design choices to determine which features were most essential to our app's user experience.

Dropping the 'Saved Places' feature (at least for now):

✔ Less distraction and decision fatigue for users
✔ Reduces development time
✘ May be important when expanding app beyond the gardens
