Creating a new space-discovery experience (in cities, not outer-space...yet!)


Overview

This is my on-going journey designing a curator-based space-discovery platform. We launched the iOS app July 2020 in Seoul. As of October 2020, it has 1000+ downloads, with 300+ WAUs.

My Role

Joining part-time in May 2020, I led the product design from the ground up. I collaborated with 3 team members who did product, development, marketing, and user research.

Background

You have a date this Friday night. How do you find a place to go? (In a COVID-free universe...)

Daytrip (born out the OurSpace startup) makes it easy to discover the perfect spots to enjoy with people you care about: partners, friends, family...and yourself! We give select content-curators a platform to share photos and info about the most special spaces in town. Besides the mobile app, DayTrip also maintains this Instagram page to highlight spaces and curators in South Korea.

Process

Prioritizing customer value for every feature

Startups go fast – even if as a designer I prefer taking my sweet time to understand who I'm designing for, and why. I had to whip out some features in mere days within 1-week sprints. Users and future business value guided the development of every feature, so I also had to prioritize which designs were left at MVP-level and which ones to revisit.

Insights

Existing solutions lack in experience and functionality for discovering high-quality physical spaces.

When we first started in May/June, David and Cherlyn did an initial round of interviews with potential users from the DayTrip Instagram. In these interviews, we wanted to understand how people used existing apps to find new places to go.


Beautiful photos matter – but they're all on Instagram.

Initially, we had a few hundred spaces with gorgeous photos taken by Cherlyn (and David). Some had been shared on Instagram, attracting over 2000 followers. Check-out the difference between the same place on Google Maps vs. in photos that were taken with skill & care:


And there are so many photographers and influencers sharing jaw-dropping photos to Instagram. They promote a lifestyle surrounding the exploration of these beautiful spaces. People love it – the comments are full of followers tagging someone, saying "let's go!".



80,000+ restaurants in Seoul. 40,000+ in Paris. 20,000+ in NYC.

South Korea's "cafe culture" is really showing here. And that's not even including all the other places like shops, museums, IG-worthy destinations. It is so overwhelming to use services that list all these places.


You probably have someone you want to go with. And where do we go next after this food-coma?

Who's looking for new spots to go the most? The lovebirds. "Where do you want to go?" "Idk." People struggle with coordinating plans and "wish lists" when they are scattered across messages and tags on social media.

One interviewee also noted how they may go to multiple spots in one outing, but it's a pain to plan out an itinerary or spontaneously find the next destination on-the-go.

Design goals

Give it the functionality of saving & searching + a whole lot of beauty. It's a brand. It's a vibe.

I value the connection between product design and branding. Insights from the preferences of Daytrip's Instagram followers (majority women in their 20s-30s) shaped the visual design decisions I made for the UI. Especially since the first touch-point for users is our Instagram page, the app had to match the elegant, kinda artsy, but still inviting vibe of the DayTrip account.

In order to support our brand, making sure the app gave users the right feeling was my top priority when I approached the MVP design. At least for our first 1000 users, they share the values we promote.

Designing for 3 scenarios

When I first started designing, I didn't formally create personas since we were still – and still always are – talking to users (and again, #StartupsAreSpeedy). I did start with these use cases in mind:

  • Immediate – "I'm out & about and want to find somewhere to go right now."
  • Near-future – "I'm looking for a new place to visit this weekend with my partner or friend."
  • Future – "I'm keeping track of all the places I want to go to someday."

Thinking about these use cases helped me contextualize app flows in a user's journey, which was helpful in making decisions about the information architecture (with tabs organized to correspond to those use-cases) shown below:

Designing the Space Feed

Creating a magazine-like browsing experience

The MVP started with an image-based feed of spaces in the first tab. Spaces are displayed one-at-a-time to emphasize the immersive effect of the photos. However, users felt this feed was too monotonous. We also noticed from analytics that people tended to stay on one tab, so other features weren't being accessed as often. To make the feed more dynamic and to better integrate the other content in our app (i.e. curations), the redesign includes different feed items.

Allowing curators to add photos and new spaces

While beauty is important for this app, efficiency is everything for curators who are repeatedly adding pieces of content. But the first iterations I designed? They were just not it. Bless Cherlyn's blood pressure for enduring it at least a hundred times before the redesign. She came up with the flow for adding photos (for spaces that already exist) and I created the flow for adding a completely new space. A sign of success is that curators (other than Cherlyn) were able to learn and use the new flow without any guidance from our team. We're still waiting for their feedback for more improvements.
