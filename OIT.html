Duke Gardens Mobile App: From Design to Development


Objective

Enhancing the Duke University visitor experience with an AR-enabled mobile app.


Timeframe

May - August 2019 (10 weeks)


Tools

Figma, InVision, XCode

My Role

Working on a team of 8 interns and 2 mentors from Duke OIT (including 1 team lead), I was responsible for the design and front-end implementation of our project. Throughout this project, I...

  • Met with Duke University stakeholders to understand business needs
  • Engaged my team in the human-centered design process by leading team brainstorming sessions
  • Created wireframes in Figma and created clickable prototypes in InVision
  • Implemented my designs in XCode and integrated back-end data to create a fully functioning iOS app
  • Discussed product strategy and designing for scale

The Opportunity

Hundreds of thousands of visitors arrive on Duke University's campus each year. Not only to watch games or tour campus as a prospective student, but to visit attractions like the Nasher Museum of Art and Sarah P. Duke Gardens. With the upcoming opening of an expensive visitor center, there's plenty of incentive to revamp the visitor experience.

We decided to make a visitor app for the Duke Gardens first. After meeting with multiple stakeholders (including Duke Athletics and one of Duke's executive VPs) , our team decided to use the gardens as a proof of concept for a visitor app that could be expanded to the rest of campus.

The Sarah P. Duke Gardens is a sprawling botanical garden open to the public, attracting over 300,000 visitors each year. It is not only a top spot to see on campus, but also the local Durham/Raleigh area.

Did someone say 'Augmented Reality'? Besides creating a visitor app, another half of our project was focused on implementing the AR technology from our project sponsor.
We dreamed up possible applications of AR in our app, however as the weeks progressed, we discovered that some of our plans were impossible with our sponsor's technology.

My opportunity was working as a "UX team of one". Although we had a team lead from Duke OIT, my team members' roles were mostly self-directed. This meant that I wanted to incorporate elements of the design process in our work, I had to shape those opportunities myself.

Research

Field visits & interviews

To begin, my team made several trips to the Duke Gardens to observe visitors, conduct interviews with gardens staff, and to experience the gardens for ourselves. One visit included a tour of the entire gardens offered by volunteer tour guides.



We interviewed gardens staff, from tour guides to the marketing director. They shared with us some of the most commonly asked questions from visitors:


"Where's the parking lot / bathroom / cafe / exit ?"

"Where should I go today?"

"How long does it take to visit?"

User personas

From observations and informal conversations with gardens visitors, I created 3 "proto-personas" to help guide the rest of our design process.

Understanding stakeholders

At this stage, we also completed our initial stakeholder meetings with the gardens and Duke's executive board. We asked about existing initatives and data, in addition to opportunities for revenue-generation. These meetings gave us guidance on which aspects of the visitor experience to focus on, in addition to the business requirements that aligned with user needs.

Ideation

How might we create a simple mobile experience that enables visitors to make the most of the Gardens?

When my team began thinking about which features to incorporate in our app, I took initiative in suggesting we use post-it notes to organize our ideas and try to create a "task analysis". For each key stage in the visitor journey, we added ideas about both what features and information a user would need.


Ideating together

To encourage participation and divergent thinking, I asked all of my team members to sketch out design ideas before discussing our ideas as a group.



Main design goals

  • An easy-to-navigate map experience
  • Encourage exploration of different points of interest in the Gardens
  • Integrate self-guided tours that balance information with real-life exploration

Wireframes

From low up to medium-fidelity

Because this project could become part of Duke's suite of mobile apps, I did not define a distinct visual design. I spoke to one of Duke OIT's UX/UI designers and learned that while OIT did not have a mobile design/pattern system, they did sometimes take cues from Material Design. I kept this in mind when deciding to use cards, drop shadows, and rounded corners.


Getting guerilla feedback

I asked several other interns at Duke OIT to walk through the wireframes and tell me what they thought each screen’s function was. Everyone seemed to have a good grasp of what the app did, although the concept of “saving” favorite locations sparked discussion. What if it could generate a tour from the saved places?

Iterations

Prototyping new features

My first set of wireframes were a simple, broad overview of the app. To explore new ideas and user flows in more detail, I created new wireframes and also a clickable prototype. Using the prototype, I asked testers to assume 2 scenarios: 1) Planning their own tour route and 2) Exploring nearby places.




Testing results pt. 2

This time the other OIT interns were more confused as they progressed through the route-planning taskflow. They were mostly confused by the concept, rather than by what they saw on the screens. This suggested that including onboarding might be necessary in order to provide users with enough information to build some sort of a conceptual model for this novel taskflow.

Shouldn't we verify with actual gardens visitors? Using other interns to test was convenient, but it didn't tell me how much actual visitors would value being able to customize their routes. However at this point my team lead asked us to start coding, and I decided to stop exploring this concept after considering the value it could bring users would not outweigh the disproportionally large amount of time/effort to develop it.

Development

Staying flexible with a 4-week time crunch

Armed with 3-days worth of XCode training, I began building our app's visual components in XCode's storyboard and writing Swift code to make use of the data my teammates prepared on the back-end.

We had to prioritize features that would contribute the most to a minimum viable product over those that would complicate and lengthen development. I questioned my previous design choices to determine which features were most essential to our app's user experience.


Dropping the 'Saved Places' feature (at least for now):

Takeaways

Knowing what's important and when to cut corners

In 10 weeks, there wasn't the luxury of progressing cleanly through the UX process like I had seen in books, online articles, and Medium posts. When we were short on time, I applied modified, shortened versions of processes that would deliver just enough value to use. Other times, especially with our lack of user testing, I learned about the drawbacks of not going for the full push and how to compromise.

Empathy for users and teammates

Teamwork makes the [UX] dream work! In order to convince my teammates that design was important, I had to step in their shoes. "How could supporting this 'design thing' help them acheive their own goals and ultimately benefit our team's work as a whole?"

Parlez-vous 'business'?

Since we were designing a new product, business value was key. The current app does not directly support revenue-generation, and this is a hurdle in terms of bringing our app to long-term/widespread production. If I were to do this again, I would consider business, user needs, and technical constraints together at the same time to understand the ways they intersected and how that would translate to a design.
